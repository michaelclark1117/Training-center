🍽️ The cold walk-in — Restaurant Owner "Maria"

Mid-afternoon, slow period. Maria is wiping down the counter when you walk in.

  • Pays $400–$500/month in fees, never closely reviewed her statement
  • Polite but skeptical — has been pitched before
  • Main fear: disruption to daily operations and hassle of switching
  • Motivated by saving money and keeping things simple
1

The introduction

"Hi Maria, my name is [Name] with [Company]. I work with local restaurants on their credit card processing — I just need 2 minutes to show you something that could save you a few hundred dollars a month. I'll keep this quick."
Mistake: Pitching before building any rapport.
2

The statement review

"Most restaurant owners I talk to are overpaying without realizing it — it's buried in the fees. Let me take a quick look at your last statement. 60 seconds and I'll tell you if there's savings there."
3

Handle switching objection

"I completely get it — we handle everything. Terminal swap takes 10 minutes, money still deposits next business day. Most owners say they wish they'd done it sooner."
4

The close

"Let me fill out a quick one-page application for a free savings analysis. No obligation, no switching yet — just gets the ball rolling. Let me get started on that now."

🔧 The cold walk-in — Auto Repair Shop Owner "Mike"

Mike is in the shop or at the front desk. He's busy, grease on his hands, and doesn't have time for salespeople.

  • Runs a 3–5 bay shop, high ticket transactions ($500–$2,000+ per job)
  • Pays significant processing fees but doesn't think about them
  • Direct and no-nonsense — gets to the point fast or loses interest
  • Motivated by numbers — show him real dollars saved, not percentages
1

The introduction

"Hey Mike, real quick — I work with auto shops in the area on their processing fees. With the size of jobs you're running, most shops I talk to are leaving $300–$500 a month on the table. I just need 2 minutes. I'll make it fast."
Mistake: Being too polished or salesy — Mike respects direct, confident, and fast.
2

The hook — use his ticket size

"Your average ticket is probably $500–$1,500 right? Every time a customer swipes, you're losing 2–3% of that to your processor. On a $1,000 job that's $20–$30 gone. Let me pull up your statement and show you exactly what you're paying."
3

Handle the busy objection

"I hear you — I'll make it fast. Show me last month's statement and I can tell you in 60 seconds if it's worth talking about. If not, I'm out the door and you never see me again."
4

The close

"Here's what I need — one-page application and your last statement. No obligation, no switching today. I'll get you a full side-by-side comparison and you decide from there. Let's take a look."

🛍️ The cold walk-in — Retail Store Owner "Sandra"

Sandra is behind the counter or on the floor. She runs a clothing, gift, or specialty retail shop and processes dozens of transactions a day.

  • High volume of small transactions — fees add up fast
  • Often unaware of how interchange fees work
  • Worried about her terminal going down during a sale
  • Motivated by reliability, simplicity, and monthly savings
1

The introduction

"Hi Sandra, I'm [Name] with [Company]. I work with retail stores in the area on their payment processing. With how many transactions you're running every day, there's a good chance you're overpaying and don't know it. I'll be quick."
Mistake: Walking in during a rush — read the room and come back during slow periods.
2

The volume angle

"With retail, it's all about volume. Even a small reduction in your rate adds up to real money at the end of the month. If you're doing 200 transactions a day and overpaying even $0.05 per swipe — that's $300 a month you're leaving on the table."
3

Handle reliability concern

"I completely understand — the last thing you want is your terminal going down on a Saturday afternoon. Our equipment has 99.9% uptime and we have 24/7 support. Most clients say the switch was smoother than they expected."
4

The close

"Let me take a look at your statement and put together a real comparison. No switching today — I just want to show you the numbers side by side. If it makes sense, great. If not, no hard feelings. Can we do that?"

The Harlow Promise

This is the most important talk-off in your entire pitch. Merchants don't trust payment processors — and they're right not to. This is how you earn it.

The full customer care talk-off — memorize this

Opening — set the tone
"But the most important thing I'm going to provide is excellent customer care."
The long game — show them you think differently
"It does me no good to sign you up today if you cancel tomorrow. If six months from now and a year from now you're still with us, that means I've done my job."
Reframe your role
"My job isn't just to earn your business — it's to keep it."
Acknowledge the industry problem
"You and I both know there are a lot of credit card companies out there waiting for me to slip up. That's why I make sure my merchants have direct access to me."
Make the commitment personal
"From 8 to 5, I'm your guy. If you need anything, you call or email me directly."
Back it up with support
"We do have 24-hour support, but I have a vested interest in making sure you're taken care of."
The close — make it personal one more time
"If you ever need anything, call me first and I'll make sure it gets handled."

Why this works

Merchants have been burned. They've heard promises before. The reason this talk-off cuts through is because it acknowledges their distrust instead of ignoring it. You're not pretending the industry is great — you're separating yourself from it.

  • "It does me no good to sign you up if you cancel tomorrow" — signals you're playing a long game, not just chasing a commission
  • "You and I both know there are a lot of companies waiting for me to slip up" — shows self-awareness and honesty about the industry
  • "From 8 to 5 I'm your guy" — makes it human and personal, not corporate
  • "Call me first" — positions you as their advocate inside the company

When to deliver this

  • After the savings analysis — once they're interested in the numbers, this closes the trust gap
  • When they're hesitating to sign — price isn't always the issue; sometimes they just don't trust the process
  • When they say "I've tried other processors before" — this directly addresses the bad experience
  • Every single time — even if they seem ready to sign, say it anyway. It reinforces confidence in their decision.

Handling "I've been burned before"

"I hear you — and honestly that's the most common thing I hear. The industry has a bad reputation and a lot of it is deserved. That's exactly why I operate differently. My merchants have my direct number. When something comes up — and eventually something always does — you're not calling an 800 number and waiting on hold. You're calling me. That's the difference."

Handling "What happens if you leave the company?"

"That's a fair question. If I ever leave, Harlow has a full support team that takes over — 24/7. But I'll be honest with you: I have a vested interest in keeping you happy because my business grows when my merchants stay. I'm not going anywhere."
The Most Powerful Talk Track in Your Arsenal

Transact for Free — Analogies That Close

Merchants are reluctant to pass the fee to their customers. These analogies eliminate that fear by showing them it's already the norm — their customers are used to it everywhere they go.

The Olive Garden opener — use this first

"Think about when you go to Olive Garden… you might spend $90 on dinner and leave an $18 tip. That server works for Olive Garden — but tipping has become the social norm. Years ago it wasn't like that. Now it would feel awkward not to tip. Transacting for free is the same evolution. 12–13 years ago processors didn't even offer these programs. Today in 2026 we have millions of transactions worth of data showing customers are completely used to it. Customers care more about convenience, speed, and service than a small processing fee. Businesses don't lose customers over a 3% convenience adjustment — they lose them over long wait times, mistakes, or bad service."
Why it works: It uses a universal experience — everyone has eaten at a restaurant and tipped. It reframes the fee as a social evolution, not a burden. And it ends with the real truth: customers leave over service, not fees.

"Costco gives better pricing when you buy in bulk. Businesses structure pricing differently based on how customers purchase. Transacting for free is just another way to structure pricing."

This is one of the most powerful things you can say — and it should come before the savings pitch. It immediately separates Harlow from Square, Stripe, and every giant processor they've heard of.

"Here's something I want you to know about Harlow — we're a small business just like you. Unlike Square or these other giant processors that have a million merchants, we only have about 5,000. Why this works: You're talking to a small business owner who knows exactly what it feels like to lose a customer. The moment you say "we feel it" — they feel it too. You've gone from salesperson to peer in one sentence.
